Education Is An Electoral Promise We Fall For

Two government education loans. Years of repayment ahead. Currency conversions that steal from you before you even start. A system that wasn't designed for your success—it was designed to extract wealth. And the worst part? You believed it would be different.

A Story Based On Experience (With Timelines Edited)

This is not one person's failure. This is what happens when a government makes electoral promises about education and then builds a system designed to trap you. The names have changed. The details have been adjusted. But the story is real and it repeats thousands of times over.

The Promise They Make

Every election cycle, the same promise. Every candidate says it:

"We will invest in your education. We will give you loans so you can study. Come back and build your country with the skills you earned."

You hear this and think: This is fair. The government believes in me. I can get out of poverty. I can build a career. I can help my family.

So you apply. You get approved. The money arrives in your bank account. In MVR. Which is good, except it's not.

Because you're about to learn that the government's promise and the government's system are two different things.

The First Loan: Undergraduate Hope

Let's say you got your first loan somewhere around 2015. For undergraduate studies abroad.

The amount doesn't matter. Let's say it's roughly 400,000 MVR. With fees included, maybe more. The government says this covers your education abroad.

You're excited. You're terrified. You're ready.

You knew the tuition would be in USD. You knew accommodation costs foreign currency. You knew everything abroad costs money in a different currency than your loan. You knew this before you applied.

But you also knew you couldn't work part-time to offset it. Many countries restrict international student work hours, or don't allow it at all. Three to four years of study means three to four years of living expenses in foreign currency, with no way to earn local money to supplement.

So you convert your MVR to USD.

This is where the system starts stealing from you.

What Happens When You Convert
-5%
Your 400,000 MVR becomes roughly 25,000 USD in theory. But banks take fees. Transfer services take fees. Receiving banks take fees. By the time the money arrives, you've lost 5-7% before paying a single dollar toward tuition. That's thousands of MVR gone. To fees. Before you even started.

The government doesn't mention this. The system wasn't designed to account for it. You just... lose money. At the moment you need it most.

For four years, you make it work. You study. You stretch the money as far as it goes. You graduate.

By 2018, you have a bachelor's degree. You're proud. You're ready to contribute.

But the job market has other plans.

Repayment Begins (Before You Even Leave University)

Six months after graduation, the loan repayment starts. But here's the trap: You're not employed yet. You're not back in Maldives yet. You don't have income.

But the government loan repayment doesn't wait for your circumstances to improve. The undergraduate loan was administered through Bank of Maldives. Automatic deductions. Around 2,500 MVR every month from your bank account.

You have no choice. The money leaves. You go into personal debt just to cover the government loan payments while you're still trying to find a job.

And you've never defaulted. Not once. Even while studying your postgraduate degree.

But you realize: I need more. A bachelor's degree isn't enough. The job market is competitive. The salaries are low. I need a master's degree to have any chance.

You apply for a second government loan. For postgraduate studies.

The government approves another one. Roughly 1.8 million MVR. Yes, million.

You think: Okay, now I'll get serious. Master's degree. Better job. I can repay both loans.

You don't yet know that the job market doesn't reward master's degrees in Maldives.

The Second Loan: Ambition Meets System

Around 2023, you're abroad again. For postgraduate studies. Same problem as before: MVR loan, foreign currency expenses. Currency conversion losses repeat. You lose money again at the point of disbursement.

Meanwhile, the undergraduate loan repayment continues. Every month, around 2,500 MVR still deducts from your account. While you're a student again. While you have no income. Not everyone gets a grace period during further study. You didn't.

You make it work somehow. Through personal loans, part-time work where allowed, stretching every penny.

You graduate with your master's degree in 2024. You have hope again. Now surely, with this degree, the salary will be better.

It's not.

The Reality Check: Graduation Meets Maldives Job Market

You finish your degree. You're qualified. You have skills. You're ready.

But here's what a master's degree actually pays in Maldives:

Master's Degree Salary in Maldives
22,000-28,000 MVR
Best case scenario, maybe 35,000 MVR if you're lucky. Your education premium over a bachelor's: roughly 5,000 MVR. After two government loans, years of repayment, currency conversion losses, personal debt to cover payments while studying—your education premium is 5,000 MVR.

You reach out to the government. You explain: My visa situation is complicated. Can the grace period on my postgraduate loan be extended? I don't have employment yet.

You're reasonable. You've never defaulted on your undergraduate loan. You're asking for a grace period.

The response comes much later. It says: No. But we're working on a leniency policy.

Working on it. For months. Maybe years. Still working on it.

Meanwhile, your first postgraduate payment is due. But here's where the system changes for you: Unlike your undergraduate loan (which was processed through Bank of Maldives), your postgraduate loan is now administered through MIRA (Maldives Inland Revenue Authority).

And MIRA's system is different. You don't pay monthly. You have to pay the full monthly amount—around 7,000+ MVR—each time you pay. And you must choose which month to pay it for. The moment you miss a month, it piles up. No penalties accumulate, but the arrears keep mounting. You get warning letters. And if the debt keeps piling, you could risk ending up on the government's name-and-shame list—you never know what they might do.

Now do the math.

You're technically insolvent before you earn your first paycheck.

You can pay the undergraduate loan automatically (you always have). But the postgraduate loan? You cannot afford the full 7,000+ MVR every month.

⚠️ The MIRA Payment Trap

Unlike the Bank of Maldives undergraduate loan (automatic, flexible), the postgraduate loan through MIRA requires you to pay the full monthly amount whenever you choose to pay. But you can only pay full months—no partial payments. Skip a month? That month's payment just piles up. You owe 7,000 MVR for January. You skip it. Now you owe 14,000 MVR for January and February. No penalties accumulate, but the arrears keep mounting. You get warning letters. And if the debt keeps piling, you could risk ending up on the government's name-and-shame list—or they might not do it, you never know.

This is not a failure of willpower. This is not poor financial management. This is mathematics that was always impossible.

The Choice They Never Told You About

Now you face a choice. It's not really a choice—it's coercion disguised as choice.

Option 1: Stay in Maldives

Pay the undergraduate loan (you always have). Default on the postgraduate loan. Watch arrears pile up because MIRA requires full monthly payments. Get warning letters from the tax authority. Could risk being added to a name-and-shame list—or they might not, you never know. Live in poverty despite having a master's degree. Cannot save. Cannot invest. Cannot have a future.

Option 2: Leave and Earn Abroad

Earn in foreign currency. Convert to MVR for loan payments (losing 4-7% on every transaction). Have a standard of living. Can eventually repay from a position of strength, not desperation.

The government calls this Option 2 "brain drain."

The government blames you for leaving. The government says you're unpatriotic.

The government doesn't acknowledge that it created the conditions that forced you to leave.

It's Not A Choice

This is important: It's not a choice between patriotism and selfishness. It's not a choice between loyalty and opportunity.

It's survival.

The government says: "Get educated. Come back. Build your country." The system says: "You're allowed to fail, but not publicly. Pay your loans. Don't complain. Don't leave. But also, there are no jobs. Good luck. And if you can't pay, we might put your name on a list for everyone to see. Or we might not. You never know."

Every educated Maldivian who leaves is making the rational choice given an impossible system. The system incentivizes departure. The system punishes return.

The government then blames them.

This is the cruelty at the heart of the system.

The Electoral Promise Revealed

Education was an electoral promise. Every candidate, every cycle, says they'll invest in education. They win votes with this promise.

Then the system you enter is designed to:

• Lose money at disbursement (currency conversion fees)

• Demand payment from jobs that don't exist (25,000 MVR salary vs 9,500 MVR loan obligation)

• Make you pay while studying (no grace period for further education)

• Change the payment system after you've already borrowed (MIRA full-payment requirement for new loans)

• Criminalize the inevitable default (warning letters and the threat of public shaming)

• Blame you for leaving (brain drain narrative)

• Pretend to fix it (working on leniency policy, for years)

The electoral promise was never about your success. It was about winning elections. You believed it. You fell for it. And now you're trapped.

Why This Matters Beyond You

This story connects to everything about why Maldives fails:

Government invests in education (makes electoral promise) → No jobs exist for educated people (Article 2: Tourism Plantation) → Graduates can't repay (system impossible) → Government cuts future education investment (Articles 1 & 3: Fiscal crisis) → Fewer people get educated → Less skilled workforce → Lower productivity → Fiscal crisis worsens → Cycle repeats

The student loan system didn't fail by accident. It failed by design. The government made a promise it never intended to back with real jobs, real wages, real opportunity.

You got educated exactly as promised. The system just never expected you to actually try to stay.

The Real Cost

Two loans. Roughly 2.2 million MVR total. Repayment spanning nearly two decades (through 2045). Currency losses at every stage. Salary that doesn't cover it. A government that acknowledges the problem but refuses to fix it. And a young person caught between staying and starving, or leaving and being blamed for it—living in uncertainty about what might happen if they default.

It's Not Weakness

If you chose to leave, you're not weak. You're not unpatriotic. You're rational. The system forced you. Every educated Maldivian abroad represents not brain drain—but a brain drain that was engineered by a system that promised opportunity and delivered debt. A system that made you pay for further education while still repaying your first loan. A system that changed the rules mid-game. A system where you live with the uncertainty of what might happen next.

The Question Remains

Why create student loans when there are no jobs? Why invest billions in education for an economy with no demand for education? The government has never answered. The government has never fixed it. And every educated Maldivian carries the weight of that failure—and the uncertainty of what comes next.
