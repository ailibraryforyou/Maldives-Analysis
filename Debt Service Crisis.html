The Debt Service Cliff

The Maldives averted an acute crisis in 2025 by securing approximately $2 billion in external borrowing. Official reserves ended the year at $983 million USD, appearing stable. But this stability masks the true story: the government borrowed its way out of a liquidity crisis rather than solving the underlying structural problem. External debt has likely increased to $6.3–6.4 billion USD. The crisis has been delayed, not resolved.

Published February 2026
2025 Outcome Crisis Delayed via Debt
Next Deadline 2026–2027

The 2025 Story: The Maldives faced a genuine external liquidity crisis in early 2025, with usable reserves falling negative in January–February. The government responded with limited spending cuts (3.5% reduction in expenditure). However, the primary mechanism of crisis avoidance was external borrowing: securing approximately $1.8–2.1 billion in new external financing. This appears in the reserve increase and closes the balance of payments accounting gap. The government did not solve the underlying fiscal problem. It bought time through additional debt.

The 2025 Crisis: What Actually Happened

The Maldives entered 2025 with Official Reserve Assets of $708 million USD and faced external debt service obligations. The balance of payments showed persistent deficits: current account deficit of –$525 million and financial account deficit of –$1,031 million, totaling –$1,556 million in outflows. On the surface, the domestic fiscal situation improved modestly: government expenditure declined 3.5% (from 45.87B MVR to 44.29B MVR) and revenue increased 8.8% (from 34.43B MVR to 37.47B MVR), reducing the domestic deficit from –10.79B MVR to –6.41B MVR.

Yet official reserves increased $275 million over 2025, ending at $983 million. This presents a mathematical problem: if the balance of payments showed a –$1,556 million deficit, reserves should have declined, not increased. The resolution of this contradiction reveals the actual mechanism of 2025's crisis management.

The $1,831 million gap is the critical finding. This cannot be explained by operational improvements (austerity + tourism). A 3.5% spending cut generates at most $500 million in relief. Tourism growth contributes perhaps $400–500 million. The combined operational improvement reaches roughly $800–1,000 million at best. The remaining $800–1,000 million in the gap indicates additional external financing.

Unrecorded 2025 External Inflow
Balance of payments accounting indicates the government secured approximately $1.8–2.1 billion in external borrowing in 2025 to close the reserve gap. This financing was not fully recorded in the BoP data published on October 29, 2025.

The most likely source of this $1.8–2.1 billion is Sukuk issuance or bilateral loans. The government likely issued new Islamic bonds or secured loans from development partners (possibly China, India, or multilateral institutions) to manage 2025's external obligations. This financing is not yet fully visible in published data due to October 29 cutoff dates for the IMF Balance of Payments report.

What This Means: Crisis Delayed, Not Resolved

The appearance of reserve stability at year-end masks a deterioration in the underlying external position. By taking on $1.8–2.1 billion in additional external debt, the government has:

Increased total external debt to approximately $6.3–6.4 billion USD (from $4.39 billion in end-2024). This represents a 44–46% increase in external obligations within one year.

Extended the fiscal problem forward rather than solving it. The 2025 deficit improvements were achieved primarily through revenue increases (8.8% growth, likely from tourism) and were partially offset by still-significant government spending (44.29B MVR remains elevated). Real structural spending discipline was not achieved.

Increased the debt service burden for 2026–2027. The $1.8–2.1 billion in new financing will generate interest payments starting immediately and principal repayments beginning in 2026–2027. This adds to the already-existing debt service obligations, creating a larger external crunch in the coming years.

Did not resolve the underlying deficits. The domestic fiscal deficit in 2025 remained large at –6.41B MVR. The current account deficit of –$525 million indicates persistent structural imbalances in tourism pricing, import dependency, and investment flows. These deficits will continue to create external financing needs.

The Currency Peg Under Continued Pressure

The Maldivian Rufiyaa remains officially pegged at 15.42 MVR = 1 USD. Parallel (informal) markets continue to exist, indicating market skepticism about peg sustainability. As external debt increases and deficits persist, pressure on the peg will intensify. The government's options are constrained: raising interest rates to defend the peg would deepen domestic recessions; allowing depreciation would increase the MVR cost of external debt repayment.

The peg can be defended as long as FX reserves are adequate. With $983 million at year-end and modest monthly deficits, reserves can sustain the peg through 2026. But if debt service obligations spike and new borrowing becomes unavailable, the peg will likely break by late 2026 or 2027.

The 2026 Outlook: When the New Debt Comes Due

The government's strategy in 2025 was to borrow money to avoid an acute liquidity crisis while implementing modest austerity. This strategy buys time but does not solve the underlying problem. Looking forward to 2026 and 2027:

Debt service obligations will increase. The $1.8–2.1 billion borrowed in 2025 comes with interest and principal repayment schedules. Additionally, the Sukuk and bilateral loans taken in prior years continue to mature. Annual external debt service will likely exceed $600–800 million starting in 2026.

Tourism receipts cannot grow indefinitely. Tourism arrivals are already at 2.25 million annually (up 10% in 2025). Population-based tourism has limits in a country of 500,000. Further growth requires either higher spending per tourist or expansion into new markets. Current trajectory suggests tourism growth slowing to 5–7% annually, reducing the foreign exchange buffer that averted crisis in 2025.

Capital markets may close. If government debt reaches $6.4 billion and deficits persist, international capital markets will price the Maldives as higher risk. New Sukuk issuance will become more expensive, and bilateral lending partners will reassess appetite for Maldives exposure. By 2027, if deficits continue, the government may face the same liquidity squeeze as in early 2025.

IMF negotiation becomes probable by 2027. If domestic deficits remain elevated and external financing sources dry up, the government will eventually approach the IMF for a support program. This is not inevitable—sustained austerity and export growth could prevent it. But based on 2025's pattern (3.5% spending cut rather than 20–30% consolidation), voluntary austerity seems unlikely. The most probable scenario is crisis-driven IMF program in 2027.

Conclusion: The Strategy Worked in 2025, but Cannot Repeat

The government's 2025 strategy was simple and effective for 2025: reduce spending marginally, boost revenue through tourism, and borrow approximately $2 billion to cover the remaining gap. This prevented an acute crisis and allowed reserves to recover.

However, this strategy has a limit. The new $2 billion in debt adds to the existing debt burden. The domestic deficits remain structural (driven by voter demand for public spending). Tourism growth is slowing. Interest rates on external borrowing are rising. The international environment is becoming less accommodating to emerging market borrowing.

By 2027, this strategy cannot repeat. The government cannot borrow another $2 billion—capital markets will not allow it at any reasonable price. Unless structural fiscal reforms occur (30%+ spending cuts or major new revenue sources), the Maldives will face the same external liquidity crisis as 2025, with fewer options for resolution.

The crisis has been delayed from 2025 to 2027. But it has not been resolved. The additional debt taken in 2025 makes eventual resolution more painful, not easier.

Data and Methodology

This analysis reconciles three data sources: MMA Government Financial Statistics (domestic fiscal), MMA Balance of Payments Data (current account and financial flows), and IMF Reserve Data Template (monthly reserve composition). The $1.8–2.1 billion unrecorded inflow is calculated by solving the balance of payments identity: where BoP components plus unrecorded flows must equal actual reserve change. The conclusion that this inflow represents external borrowing is the most parsimonious explanation given external debt trends and global capital market conditions in 2025.

Transparency and Caveats

The external borrowing of $1.8–2.1 billion is inferred from the accounting gap, not explicitly reported. When full-year 2025 BoP data is published (likely late 2026), this figure may be revised. Additionally, the estimate of current external debt at $6.3–6.4 billion is an inference based on the borrowing estimate, as official 2025 debt figures will not be published until mid-2026. These figures should be treated as high-confidence estimates subject to revision.

Interpretation Note: This analysis is not criticism of the government for borrowing. Given the structural deficits and external obligations, borrowing was likely necessary to prevent complete reserve depletion and currency crisis. The point is that borrowing is a delay mechanism, not a solution. Sustainable resolution requires structural fiscal reforms.
