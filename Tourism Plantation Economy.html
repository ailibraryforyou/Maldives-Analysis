The Tourism Plantation Economy

Why 75% growth in tourists brought almost no benefit to ordinary Maldivians—and how the tourism sector became an extractive economy that enriches foreigners while impoverishing the state

Published February 2026
Topic Tourism Value Capture
Data Source MMA Tables 3.1–3.3

The Paradox: Tourist arrivals are up 75% (2020–2025). Government revenue from tourism is flat or declining. More people visiting. Less money staying in country. This is not coincidence—it is the structure of the Maldives tourism model.

The Growth That Isn't

The official narrative is clear: tourism is booming, more tourists arrive every year, and record-breaking numbers are celebrated in headlines. But this narrative masks a darker reality that the numbers reveal only when examined carefully. The Maldives is not benefiting proportionally from tourism growth because ownership and profit capture are concentrated overseas. While arrivals have increased dramatically, the actual value flowing back to the government and local economy has stagnated.

The Decline That Reveals Everything

A single metric tells the complete story: average tourist stay has declined from 7.6 days (2021) to 6.1 days (2025). With relatively stable per-night spending rates, fewer nights per tourist equals less revenue per visitor. This is the hidden truth behind the headline numbers. The government celebrates record arrivals while missing the essential fact that each tourist is spending less time in the country.

Why is average stay declining? The answer lies in market dynamics. Budget resorts are proliferating, creating intense price competition. Weekend trips are becoming more common than week-long vacations. The exclusivity of Maldives tourism—once a luxury destination—has eroded as more properties focus on volume rather than value. The market is saturating at the budget end, attracting price-conscious travelers who stay shorter periods. Rather than protecting margins and average stay lengths, resorts compete on price, attracting the wrong market segment.

The fiscal implication is profound: the government collects the same or less revenue from more tourists because per-person spending has declined. The headline growth masks the underlying deterioration in per-capita tourism revenue.

The Plantation Economy Model

Understanding the Structure

The Maldives operates a tourism economy that is structurally identical to the plantation economies of the colonial era. In traditional plantations, foreign investors owned the productive assets (land and equipment), employed local labor at subsistence wages, extracted resources, and sent profits overseas. The colonial government received minimal tax revenue while local populations saw little economic benefit despite living in a resource-rich territory.

The modern Maldives tourism economy follows the exact same pattern. Foreign investors own 70% of the resorts that capture 75% of all tourism bednights. These resorts employ Maldivian workers at or near minimum wage to staff the properties. The resorts then extract profits and repatriate them overseas through dividend payments, intercompany pricing, and capital repatriation. The Maldives government, despite hosting the productive capacity that generates tourism revenue, captures only a fraction of the value created.

How Value Is Extracted

Consider a typical foreign-owned resort transaction. A tourist pays $200 per night for accommodation. The resort's actual cost structure is straightforward: $80 for imported food, water, utilities, and supplies; $30 for labor (Maldivian workers on minimum wage); leaving $90 per night as operational profit. Of this $90, perhaps $10 might be paid as property tax to the government. The remaining $80 per night—multiplied across hundreds of rooms—flows out of Maldives as profit to overseas shareholders.

A local guest house, by contrast, charges $80 per night. Operating costs are $30, leaving $50 per night as profit that stays with the Maldivian owner. However, guest houses control only 15% of the market. The math is inescapable: because foreign-owned resorts dominate the market and repatriate most profits, the Maldives tourism economy enriches overseas investors far more than it benefits the nation.

This is precisely how plantation economies function. The colonial power (now foreign investors) controls production. Labor is local and undercompensated. Resources are extracted and profits leave the territory. The local government receives minimal tax revenue relative to the economic activity occurring. The result is an economy that appears dynamic and growing while local populations see little improvement in their economic circumstances, and government coffers remain perpetually empty despite enormous tourism activity.

The Tax Collection Problem

The Maldives government attempts to tax tourism through a 9% tourism tax on room rates and 10% GST on services. In theory, this should generate substantial revenue from the $5–6 billion annual tourism sector. In practice, tax collection is minimal. The government collects approximately 800 million MVR annually from tourism—less than 2% of total government revenue. This is extraordinarily low for a country where tourism represents 60% of exports and the primary economic activity.

Why is tax revenue so low relative to economic activity? Foreign-owned resorts negotiate tax exemptions as a condition of investment. They employ transfer pricing strategies to shift profits to overseas parent companies, minimizing taxable income in Maldives. They allocate costs aggressively to reduce reported profits. The government lacks capacity to audit large resorts effectively. And critically, major resorts have political protection that shields them from rigorous tax enforcement. A small government cannot risk antagonizing the large foreign investors upon which economic activity depends.

The result is a two-tier system: foreign investors profit enormously while the government struggles to fund basic services. This is the essence of extractive economics. The presence of tourism activity—the helicopters, the resorts, the thousands of workers—creates an illusion of prosperity. But the actual flow of wealth is overwhelmingly outbound, leaving the state fiscally weak despite economic activity that would ordinarily be sufficient to fund a functioning government.

Why This Explains the Fiscal Crisis

The persistent fiscal crisis in Maldives cannot be understood without recognizing that tourism, while dominating the economy, contributes minimally to government finances. The structural problem is not that tourism is declining or that the economy is shrinking. The problem is that the tourism economy is organized in a way that extracts value from Maldives rather than capturing it for the state.

Tourism grows. Headlines celebrate record arrivals. But government revenue stagnates because foreign investors control the productive capacity and repatriate profits. Per-tourist spending declines because resorts compete on price rather than value. Tax collection remains low because the political economy prevents aggressive taxation of major investors. The result is an economy that looks like it is booming but leaves the government unable to fund basic operations.

This explains why the fiscal deficit persists at 11–13% of GDP despite tourism being the primary economic activity. Growth in the tourism sector does not translate to government revenue growth because most tourism value is captured by overseas interests. The government is forced to borrow to make up the gap between spending needs and actual revenue. The debt accumulates. Interest costs rise. External payment obligations mount. Eventually, the fiction that growth solves everything collapses into crisis.

The Maldives is trapped in a structure that was designed—whether intentionally or through the path-dependent logic of tourism development—to benefit foreign investors. The nation has the tourism. It simply does not keep the profits. This is the plantation economy.

Conclusion

The Maldives tourism economy is booming in terms of visitor arrivals and activity levels. But beneath the surface metrics that governments tout, a structural economic problem persists. The tourism sector is organized in a way that extracts maximum value for foreign investors while leaving the Maldives government chronically underfunded despite the presence of enormous economic activity.

Seventy-five percent growth in tourists over five years should have solved the fiscal crisis. Instead, the deficit deepens. The fiscal deficit cannot be closed by tourism growth because tourism growth does not reach the government. The sector is organized as an extractive economy serving overseas interests first, and the Maldivian state second.

Until this structural problem is addressed—either through higher taxation of foreign investors, requirements for local ownership, or diversification away from foreign-dominated resort tourism—tourism growth will remain a mirage. More activity at the resort level. Less benefit at the national level. A tourism economy that looks prosperous from the outside while leaving the state perpetually in crisis.

This is the tourism plantation economy. The colonizers are gone, but the economic model remains.
