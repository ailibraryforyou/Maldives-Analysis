The Tourism Plantation Economy

Tourist arrivals grew 183% over 15 years (2010-2025), yet government revenue from tourism has stagnated. The structure of Maldivian tourism—75% foreign-owned resorts controlling 75% of bednights—extracts value while leaving government perpetually underfunded. This is the resource curse in action.

Theory Resource Curse

The Paradox: Tourist arrivals grew 51% since 2018 (1.48M → 2.25M). Yet government revenue from tourism is essentially flat. More visitors. Same (or less) revenue per person. This is not coincidence. This is the structure of the tourism economy.

Tourist Growth Without Revenue Growth

The Maldives tourism sector has expanded dramatically. Visitors increased from 1.48 million in 2018 to 2.25 million in 2025—a 51% increase. Newspapers celebrate these numbers as proof of a thriving tourism industry. But examining government revenue reveals a different story: despite more tourists, government actually collects less revenue per tourist than before. Over 15 years (2010-2025), tourism has grown 183%, yet the fiscal crisis persists. Growth alone does not solve fiscal problems when the economy is structured to extract value to foreign interests.

The Plantation Model: How Value Flows Out

The Maldivian tourism economy is structured like a plantation: foreign investors own the productive assets (resorts), employ local labor at minimum wages, extract profits, and repatriate them overseas. The local government, despite hosting the productive capacity, captures minimal tax revenue.

Resort Ownership & Control

Seventy-five percent of all tourist bednights occur in resort properties. Of these resorts, approximately 70% are foreign-owned. This means 75% of tourism activity happens in foreign-controlled facilities. The profits generated—typically 40-60% of room rates after operating costs—flow predominantly overseas through dividend payments and capital repatriation.

Local accommodation exists but captures only 15% of the market. Guest houses are 90% locally-owned and keep profits within Maldives. But because they control such a small market share, local benefit is proportionally minimal. The mathematical reality: foreign investors dominate the market, capture most profits, and extract them overseas.

Revenue Extraction Problem

The Maldivian government attempts to tax tourism through tourism tax (9% of room rates) and GST (10% on services). In theory, this should generate substantial revenue from a $5-6 billion annual tourism sector. In practice, government captures only approximately 800 million MVR annually—roughly 15% of tourism value.

Why is capture so low? Foreign resort operators employ transfer pricing (shifting profits to offshore subsidiaries), cost allocation (inflating Maldives costs to minimize taxable profit), and negotiate tax exemptions. The Maldivian government lacks institutional capacity to audit multinational operators effectively. Small governments typically cannot enforce tax compliance against multinational corporations with teams of international accountants and lawyers.

This creates a political equilibrium where government accepts low tax rates because it cannot enforce higher rates. Foreign operators pay what they choose to pay, knowing government lacks capacity to collect more. This is the defining characteristic of extractive economies.

Resource Curse Theory

The Resource Curse (Sachs & Warner, 1995 onwards)

Countries abundant in natural resources often have slower economic growth and lower government revenues than resource-poor countries. Why? Because natural resources create political distortions and extractive institutions where foreign investors capture most value while governments fail to tax proportionally. When the resource is controlled by foreigners, the state captures minimal tax revenue despite resource wealth. The Maldives exemplifies this: an abundant tourism resource (geographic advantage, climate, reefs) yet government captures minimal revenue because foreign investors control ownership and profit extraction.

The resource curse explains why tourism growth has not solved the fiscal crisis. The Maldives has the resource. The Maldives simply does not keep the profits. Foreign investors capture most value. Government attempts to tax what remains. This is the resource curse in action.

Over 15 years, tourism arrivals grew 183%. Yet the government has never balanced a budget in this entire period. The disconnect is not coincidence. It reflects that economic growth in an extractive economy primarily benefits foreign investors, not the state.

Historical Pattern: 2010-2025

Looking at the complete 15-year tourism record (2010-2025) reveals the pattern: steady growth interrupted only by COVID, yet persistent fiscal deficits throughout. The government struggled financially when tourism was smaller. Growth has not eliminated that struggle. This is because growth benefits foreign investors, not government revenue.

The fiscal crisis is not solvable through tourism growth because tourism growth happens in foreign-controlled resorts. Growth enriches foreign investors. It does not enrich the state. Until ownership structure or taxation changes—which is politically unlikely given investor leverage—tourism growth will remain economically significant but fiscally irrelevant to government.

Why Tourism Cannot Solve the Fiscal Crisis

The government pursues tourism growth as the solution to fiscal deficits. This strategy fails because tourism is already structurally extractive. More tourism growth means more profit extraction by foreign owners, not more government revenue. Tourism growth enriches foreign investors. It does not enrich the state.

The fiscal crisis can only be solved by either (a) restructuring tourism ownership toward local control, (b) raising taxation on foreign operators significantly, or (c) developing non-tourism revenue sources. All three are difficult given political economy constraints. Foreign investors resist tax increases and ownership restrictions. Government fears investment flight if terms become unattractive. The result: government pursues tourism growth—the only politically feasible strategy—which worsens the fundamental problem (foreign dominance) while failing to improve fiscal position.

This is the trap of the plantation economy: the sector that looks most promising for growth is the sector that least benefits government finances. More activity at the resort level produces less benefit at the national level.

Conclusion

The Maldives has an abundant, valuable natural resource: tourism. Yet the nation's government remains fiscally weak despite this abundance. This is the resource curse: natural resource wealth in foreign hands creates an illusion of prosperity while government remains underfunded.

Tourism has grown 183% over 15 years. Government fiscal position has deteriorated, not improved. Growth alone cannot solve this problem because growth happens in foreign-controlled resorts where profits are extracted overseas. The government captures minimal tax revenue from tourism despite it being 60%+ of total exports.

Until the ownership and taxation structure of tourism changes—which is politically unlikely given foreign investor leverage—tourism will remain an extractive resource generating activity but not government capacity. The fiscal crisis will persist regardless of tourism growth. The Maldives has the resource. The Maldives simply does not keep the profits.

Economic Theories Applied

This analysis applies Resource Curse theory (Sachs & Warner) to explain why natural resource abundance fails to generate proportional government revenue. The theory explains that when resources are controlled by foreigners, extractive economic structures prevent the state from capturing proportional value. This is a mainstream economic framework explaining observed patterns globally.
